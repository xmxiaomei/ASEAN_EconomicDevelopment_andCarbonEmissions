Who is breaking the link between growth and carbon?

Real GDP keeps climbing across Southeast Asia. Emissions don't have to. This pulls the World Bank API live and asks, country by country, whether output is pulling away from CO₂ — and who pollutes less than their income would predict.

How this is built

Everything here is computed in your browser from four World Bank series, fetched live on load. Nothing is hard-coded, so the numbers move when the World Bank updates.

Series pulled

Real GDP NY.GDP.MKTP.KD · GDP per capita, real NY.GDP.PCAP.KD · CO₂ total, excl. LULUCF EN.GHG.CO2.MT.CE.AR5 · CO₂ per capita EN.GHG.CO2.PC.CE.AR5. The CO₂ series is the EDGAR-sourced GHG release the World Bank now publishes, which covers every country annually with a short lag.

Decoupling classification

Absolute = GDP rose while CO₂ fell. Relative = both rose but CO₂ slower (intensity falling). None = CO₂ kept pace or outran GDP.

The four metrics

Decoupling gap = GDP change − CO₂ change over the window. Intensity trend = annualised change in CO₂ per dollar of GDP (negative is good). Composite = weighted average of z-scores you control. Residual = each country's distance from a robust Theil–Sen fit (median of pairwise slopes) of CO₂ per capita on log GDP per capita across all ten.

Cautions built in

Each country uses its latest year where GDP and CO₂ both exist; mismatched or pre-2021 latest years are flagged. CO₂ data lags 2–3 years, so the "latest" is rarely the calendar year. Ten countries is a small sample, so the line uses a robust Theil–Sen fit that resists being tilted by leverage points like Brunei — but treat the residual as a conversation-starter, not a verdict.
